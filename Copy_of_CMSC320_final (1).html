Introduction

In this tutorial, we want to explore potential relationships between various housing market factors in San Francisco. In order to do so, we will analyze building permit data in San Francisco between 2013 and 2018. We aim to explore questions such as, "Are there any factors that affect building permit completion?" and "Are certain permit types commonly issued at similar times?".

San Francisco and the state of California are in the midst of one of the most acute housing crises in the country, and the city is notorious globally for its very slow increases in year-over-year housing supply; some estimates suggest at the current rate of housing permit approvals, it would take the city decades to meet the shortage of housing units today.

This tutorial will guide you through a dataset of the city's building permits to develop a model that will predict the likelihood of a permit approvalas well as to learn how different factors relate to the status of a permit. These permits include more than just housing construction, but they can provide insight into how the city approaches permits, and where there is room for policy improvement.

To begin, import Building_Permits.csv from the Kaggle webpage.

def generate_shades(base_color, num_shades):
    base_color_rgb = np.array(mcolors.to_rgb(base_color))
    shades = [tuple(base_color_rgb * (1 - 0.5 * i / num_shades)) for i in range(num_shades)]
    return shades

Developing the model

We want to determine how various independent factors we've cherry-picked in previous sections will impact the likelihood of a permit being approved. The neighborhood or existing use of land, for instance, could potentially impact the probability.

Because we are dealing with independent factors and trying to find a single probability, linear regression is a reasonable model. It tries to find a relationship between input factors and output a single numeric value based on the input values.

The independent factors that the model will be trained on are called features.

Many features are categorical, meaning they fall into discrete categories (as opposed to something like continuous numerical data), and the categories are strings.

Models might learn from the characters in these strings instead of the actual underlying data they're intended to represent. To address this issue, categorical data must be converted into discrete binary columns in a process called One-Hot Encoding.

If you've never learned about One-Hot Encoding before, check out this well-written article.

Use sklearn's OneHotEncoder to encode the categorical variables, which are all the features except the creation date, since time is continuous.

Why are zip codes categorical? While these numbers may be continuous, an increase or decrease in zip codes doesn't reveal anything about the neighborhood; a zip code is essentially a neighborhood described by a number, just like how neighboroods are described by strings/words in everyday life.

Now that the categorical data is in a format that works better for machine learning models, we'll need to fix the "Permit Creation Date" column. Dates formatted as MM/DD/YYYY are strings, so like the categorical variables we just encoded, the machine learning model shouldn't be trained with this data.

Dates are not categorical thougha higher date (converted into a continous number like months or days) signifies a later point in time, and an increase or decrease in the number means something. However, day might be less relevant; based on how permits approvals work in the U.S., we can safely say the day relative to the start of a month is unlikely to really impact what happens to the permit.

Convert this column into a column of two integer columns, "Permit Creation Month" and "Permit Creation Year." Be sure to remove the original column once you're finished.

For help: https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/reference/api/pandas.Series.dt.year.html and https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/reference/api/pandas.Series.dt.month.html
